Everyone Built the Directory. Almost Nobody Built This.

Scholarship search sites are everywhere — discovery is a crowded, low-value layer. The part of paying for college almost nobody helps with is the action that happens after you already have an award letter: reviewing your numbers and asking the school to reconsider.

Financial aid offices have a formal process for this — usually called a "professional judgment" review — to account for circumstances the FAFSA doesn't capture: a job loss, medical bills, or a better offer from a comparable school. It's a normal, expected part of the system. The tools below help you understand your numbers, file the right forms, make the ask, and plan how to cover whatever's left.

Common Questions

Is it normal to appeal a financial aid award?
+
Yes. Financial aid offices expect and process appeals every year through what's typically called a "professional judgment review." It's a standard part of the system, not an unusual request — though every school's process and decision is at its own discretion.
Which tool should I start with?
+
Start with the SAI Decoder to understand your federal financial need, and the CSS Profile vs FAFSA Decoder to make sure you're filing the right forms. Once you have an award letter, use the Gap Planner to see what's left to cover, the Appeal Letter Generator to ask for more aid, and the Parent PLUS True Cost calculator before taking any parent loan.
